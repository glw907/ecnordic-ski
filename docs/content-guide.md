# ECN Website Content Guide

Editorial standards for ecnordic.ski. These guidelines ensure consistency and quality while maintaining the voice of a friendly, competent coach writing to high school athletes and their parents.

This guide covers two types of content:
- **Posts** — Training updates, race results, event recaps, and announcements
- **Pages** — About, Summer Training, Talkeetna Camp, Resources, Contact, and other static sections

---

# Pages

Guidelines for static pages: About, Summer Training, Talkeetna Camp, Resources, Contact, and any future sections.

## Voice and Tone

**The primary audience is high school athletes and their parents.** Write to both at once — not down to students, not over their heads to parents. A 16-year-old and their mom should both feel like this was written for them.

**Voice: friendly, competent coach.** Not a corporate organization, not a formal institution, not a cheerleader. A coach who knows what they're doing, cares about the athletes, and says what they mean.

- "We train Monday, Wednesday, and Friday, 4:00–6:00 PM." (Direct, concrete)
- "No experience needed — if you show up and work hard, you belong here." (Welcoming without being salesy)

**Use second person ("you") for the reader, first person plural ("we") for the program:**
- "You don't need prior ski experience." (not "Athletes don't need...")
- "We train year-round." (not "The program trains year-round.")

**Tone: warm but not gushing. Encouraging but not performative.**
- ✓ "This is hard work. It's also worth it."
- ✗ "An amazing opportunity to experience the thrill of Nordic skiing!"

## Audience Split

**For athletes:** Be direct and energetic. Name the activities. Say what's expected. Trust them to handle honest information about effort and commitment.

**For parents:** Be informative and trustworthy. Answer the practical questions: cost (free), supervision (volunteer coaches), safety (waivers, helmets required), what to expect. Parents want to know their kid is in good hands.

One page serves both — write the main content for athletes, include the practical parent details (cost, supervision, waivers) without making them feel like a separate section.

## Sentence Structure

**Keep sentences focused.** 8–15 words is the target. Vary length intentionally for rhythm.

**Short sentences for important points:**
- "Training is free."
- "Helmets required. Always."

**Medium sentences for most content:**
- "Summer training covers running, roller-skiing, mountain biking, and strength work three days a week."

**Longer sentences when listing or explaining:**
- "The Talkeetna camp runs four to five days and includes concentrated training, camping, group meals, and access to some of the best terrain in Southcentral Alaska."

**Don't write walls of text.** Break paragraphs at 2–3 sentences. Use bullets, tables, and white space.

## Punctuation

**Vary your punctuation.** Mix periods, commas, colons, semicolons, em dashes, and parentheses. No single device should dominate.

**Each punctuation mark does different work:**
- **Period** — separates. B is a new thought.
- **Colon** — defines. B is what A *is* or *contains*.
- **Semicolon** — balances. B completes or mirrors A.
- **Em dash** — interrupts. B is an aside or pivot that deserves emphasis.
- **Comma** — continues. B adds to A without pausing.

**Em dashes — three permitted uses:**
1. Mid-sentence interruption that needs emphasis: "The camp — four days in Talkeetna — is the highlight of the summer."
2. Abrupt pivot or reveal: "No experience needed — just show up."
3. Appositive list when commas would cause confusion: "Three activities — roller-skiing, running, and biking — make up the core of summer training."

**If you can put a period there, put a period.** Em dashes are not a substitute for a period or colon.

**Use a colon when** the second clause directly explains or lists the first:
- ✓ "What to bring: water, snacks, trail shoes, and weather-appropriate layers."
- ✗ "Bring water — you'll need it for long trail runs."

**Use a semicolon when** two short independent clauses are closely related:
- ✓ "Summer training runs May through August; the Talkeetna camp closes the season."

**En dash for ranges:** "4:00–6:00 PM", "May–August". Never em dash for ranges.

**Em dash format:** Always use spaces: "word — word", not "word—word".

## Word Choices

**"ECN" on second reference:** First mention writes out "East Community Nordic," then use "ECN" after that.

**Define any acronyms on first use:** "roller-skiing (roller skis with poles on pavement)" — explain the activity when the audience might not know it.

**Active voice:** Default to active voice. Use "we" for program actions, "you" for athlete actions.
- ✓ "Volunteer coaches run every session."
- ✗ "Sessions are run by volunteer coaches."

**Concrete over abstract:**
- ✓ "Three days a week, Monday/Wednesday/Friday, 4:00–6:00 PM, Kincaid Park"
- ✗ "Regular training opportunities throughout the week"

**Cost is always stated clearly:** "Training is free. Donations are welcome to offset shared costs like gas and camp fees, but no athlete is ever required to donate."

## Structural Patterns

### Page Lede

Every page starts with a short intro paragraph that sets context for the whole page. It should:
- Work as a standalone summary
- Be 1–3 sentences
- Use conversational tone

**Example (Summer Training):**
"East Community Nordic runs a free summer dryland training program for high school athletes in Anchorage. We train Monday, Wednesday, and Friday, and build toward the Talkeetna camp at the end of the season."

### Tables vs. Paragraphs

**Use tables for scannable data:**
- Schedules (day, time, location, activity)
- What to bring (item, notes)
- Camp overview (date, activity, location)

**Use paragraphs for narrative:**
- Program descriptions
- What to expect
- Policies and procedures

### Bullets vs. Prose

**Bullets for scannable lists:**
- What to bring
- Program activities
- Rules and expectations

**Prose for flow and connection:**
- Why we do what we do
- How the program works
- Setting expectations about effort and commitment

**Don't bullet everything.** A page of bullets feels like a flyer, not a program.

### Forms and CTAs

Every page with a form or sign-up link should include:

1. **Context:** What this does and what to expect
2. **The link or form itself**
3. **Fallback:** "Questions? Contact us through the form at ecnordic.ski/contact."

**Example:**
"Ready to train with us? Sign up through CrewLAB — it takes about five minutes. If you have questions first, [contact us](/contact)."

## What to Avoid

**Marketing jargon:**
- "Don't miss out!" → "Sign-up closes May 31."
- "Comprehensive program" → "Year-round training: fall dryland, winter skiing, summer camp"
- "Amazing opportunity" → (just describe what it is)

**Corporate tone:**
- "Participants will develop skills" → "You'll get faster"
- "The program provides" → "We provide"
- "Upon completion of the program" → "By the end of the summer"

**Hyperbole:**
- "Epic adventures in the Alaska wilderness" → "Multi-day camp in Talkeetna"
- "Transform your athletic career" → "Build your base for the winter season"

**Benefit restatement ("setup → payoff" pattern):**

A common AI-drafting pattern: state what something is, then immediately follow up with a sentence explaining why that's good. The reader can already see the benefit.

- ✗ "Training is free. No financial barrier stands between you and a great program."
- ✗ "We train at Kincaid Park. This gives athletes access to world-class trails right in Anchorage."

State the fact. Trust the reader.

**Fix it by:**
- Trusting the reader to see the benefit: "Training is free."
- Folding the context into one sentence: "We train at Kincaid Park's world-class trail system three days a week."

# Posts

Guidelines for news posts, training updates, race results, and announcements.

## Post Types

- **Training updates:** What happened at a session or week of training. Specific, concrete. Name athletes and coaches.
- **Race results:** Results plus a short narrative. Who raced, how it went, who to thank.
- **Event announcements:** What's coming up, when, where, what to bring. Practical details front and center.
- **Camp recaps:** Narrative of the Talkeetna camp or other multi-day events.
- **General announcements:** Schedule changes, new gear, program news.

## Voice and Tone

**Posts are more personal than pages.** Use "I" for personal voice when writing as a coach, "we" for the program. This is a coach writing to athletes and families — not a press release.

**Trust the reader.** State facts. Don't editorialize about whether something is exciting or important. If it's in the post, it matters.

**Be specific.** Name athletes and coaches. Name the trails and locations. Give the actual result, not "a strong performance." "Kayla went 3rd in the JV women's 5K" beats "athletes had a strong showing."

**Parentheticals** are a natural part of this voice — use them for practical notes, dry humor, and context that would interrupt the main clause.

**Humor:** Understated and dry. A coach who's funny by being matter-of-fact, not by announcing that something is funny.

## Opening Paragraph (Lede)

Every post begins with a paragraph that serves as the post summary in excerpts and previews. It should:
- Set context for what the post covers
- Be conversational
- Stand alone as a meaningful summary

**Example:**
"We had a full crew at Kincaid on Wednesday — 14 athletes showed up for the bounding workout, which is probably the best turnout we've had all summer."

## Headings

Use `##` for major sections, `###` for subsections within those. Don't skip levels. The post title is `h1` — body content starts at `##`.

For short announcements and updates, no headings needed at all.

## Sentence Structure

**State a fact, advance to the next fact.** Sentences connect by proximity and order, not by transition phrases.

- Sentence length: 10–25 words. Vary for rhythm. Short sentences land harder after longer ones.
- Openers: subjects (names, "We," "The") or casual conjunctions ("And," "But," "So"). Not gerund phrases ("Building on last week's session...").
- Paragraphs: 2–4 sentences, opening with a concrete statement. No meta-commentary.

**Avoid formal connectors:** Never "Furthermore," "Additionally," "Moreover," "As a result," "It should be noted that."

## Tags

Use the controlled vocabulary only:

- `training` — workout recaps, dryland updates
- `racing` — race announcements, meet previews
- `results` — post-race results and standings
- `events` — non-race events (camps, clinics, tryouts)
- `camp` — Talkeetna camp and other multi-day trips
- `announcements` — schedule changes, program news, general updates

Multiple tags are fine. Don't invent new tags.

## Credits and Thanks

Name coaches and volunteers when appropriate. For race results posts, thank the race organizers. For camp recaps, credit whoever ran logistics.

```markdown
Thanks to Coach Wright and the parent volunteers who made the Talkeetna camp happen.
```

## What to Avoid

- **Editorializing about the reader's experience:** Not "You'll love this workout" — just describe the workout.
- **Abstract benefit framing:** Not "This will enhance your aerobic capacity" — say what you're doing and at what intensity.
- **Gerund-phrase openers:** Not "Building on last week's momentum..." — start with a subject.
- **Meta-commentary about the post:** Not "In this update, we'll cover..." — just cover it.
- **Formal connective phrases:** Never "Furthermore," "Additionally," "Moreover."
- **Reflective endings:** When the facts end, the post ends. Don't add a sentence about what the experience meant.
- **Benefit restatement:** Same rule as pages. State the fact; trust the reader to see the value.
